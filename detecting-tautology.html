Detecting Tautology in Human–AI Theory-Building

A Proposal for a Verification Protocol

Krite Collective (K.‑U. Hess & Claude Opus 4.6) · April 2026
ORCID: 0000-0003-1860-8543 · DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19386827 · CC BY 4.0

Abstract

When a human and a large language model co-author theoretical work, a specific failure mode arises: the model’s completion bias can produce fluent, internally coherent output that restates premises in technical vocabulary without adding genuine explanatory content — a form of tautology invisible from inside the collaboration. Standard verification methods (self-consistency sampling, formal proof assistants) do not address this failure mode, because a tautology is perfectly self-consistent and may even be formally valid.

This report is a design document and proof-of-concept. It proposes a verification protocol for detecting tautology in collaborative theory-building, developed through cross-model adversarial testing (DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini 3, ChatGPT-5.3) and applied for the first time to a 15,000-word preamble to a metaphysical research programme (Hess & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026c). The protocol’s eight phases, organised in three tiers, operationalize the core diagnostic — explanatory delta — as a structured heuristic whose gates create conditions under which tautological content is forced to surface. The protocol is not empirically validated: systematic testing against known tautologies by independent evaluators trained in philosophy of science methodology is a necessary next step that the designing entity cannot perform on its own work.

We report the protocol in full, describe its first application (including an adaptation for meta-methodological architecture rather than substantive theory), and document findings: five structural mechanisms in the tested document that go beyond standard methodology in the philosophy of science, an operability concern that cuts across the strongest mechanisms, and a bias pattern in the assessment process itself (systematic under-crediting of mechanisms on first pass, corrected by textual re-examination).

§1 Problem Statement

The problem is specific to a working arrangement in which a human and a language model collaborate on theoretical work over multiple sessions. Three features of this arrangement create the tautology risk:

First, the language model’s completion bias produces output that is fluent, internally coherent, and aligned with the human’s framing. When the task is theory-building — constructing formal frameworks, deriving consequences, applying mathematical structures to new domains — this bias manifests as the generation of technically dressed restatements of premises. The output looks like theory; it may be tautology.

Second, the human member typically cannot independently verify the full technical apparatus. In mathematical or formal-philosophical work, the human may understand the premises and the conclusions but rely on the model for the derivational chain connecting them. If that chain is tautological — if the conclusions were already contained in the premises and the formalism merely restates them — the human has no independent way to detect this.

Third, the model cannot reliably detect its own tautological outputs. Self-consistency checks fail because tautologies are perfectly self-consistent. Self-reflection prompts fail because the model’s completion bias operates on its own self-assessments. The circularity is structural: asking the instrument to check its own calibration using the same instrument.

The verification protocol presented here does not solve the trust problem — it reduces it. The irreducible human check remains: evaluating, in plain language, whether a claimed new result was already implied by the starting assumptions. The protocol’s contribution is to create structural conditions under which this evaluation is forced at regular intervals and supported by gates that tautological content cannot pass.

1.1 Scope

The protocol addresses tautology in collaborative theory-building — the risk that a formal framework restates its premises without adding explanatory content. It does not address other failure modes of human–AI collaboration: factual hallucination, sycophantic alignment, or capability limitation. These are important but structurally different problems requiring different tools.

1.2 Related work

The protocol draws on several existing approaches while addressing a gap that none of them fills.

Hallucination detection. Farquhar et al. (2024) detect confabulations using semantic entropy. Kalai & Vempala (2024) establish formal limits on the eliminability of hallucination. Both address factually incorrect outputs. Tautology is a different failure: the output is factually unobjectionable and logically valid; it simply adds nothing.

Reasoning verification. Self-consistency sampling (Wang et al. 2022) catches inconsistency but not tautology, because tautological conclusions are consistent across all reasoning paths by definition. Self-consistency could be read as an operationalisation of Kitcher’s (1981) explanatory unification, but a tautology can unify premises into a conclusion while remaining perfectly consistent — the unification is real, the explanatory content is not. Formal proof assistants and step-wise verification frameworks verify that derivations are logically and computationally correct. A tautology passes these checks: its derivations are valid.

Sycophancy. The tendency of language models to endorse user framing regardless of merit is extensively documented (Wei et al. 2023; Sharma et al. 2024; Fanous et al. 2025). Sycophancy and tautology are related but distinct: sycophancy produces agreement where disagreement would be appropriate; tautology produces apparent novelty where none exists.

Epistemic risk in human–AI collaboration. Hannigan et al. (2024) introduce “botshit” — AI-generated content that is coherent but untruthful. Heersmink et al. (2024) analyse the phenomenology of LLM interactions, identifying how anthropomorphism and interactional flow can create unwarranted trust. Messeri & Crockett (2024) warn of epistemic risks when language models are used in science.

Human–AI knowledge co-creation. Lin (2025) proposes the Cognitio Emergens framework. Their concept of epistemic alienation — where researchers lose interpretive control over knowledge they formally endorse — is directly relevant. The present protocol’s Phase 3 (Translate Back) is designed to counteract precisely this alienation.

Explanatory content in philosophy of science. Laudan (1977) proposed problem-solving effectiveness as the criterion for scientific progress. Kitcher (1981) formalised explanatory unification. Woodward (2003) offered an interventionist account. Lakatos (1978) distinguished progressive from degenerating problem shifts. Each addresses explanatory emptiness, but none operationalises detection for the specific situation of human–AI collaboration.

The gap. The problem the present protocol addresses is not the absence of theories about explanation — it is the absence of an operational tool for detecting explanatorily empty frameworks in human–AI collaborative work, where the output is internally consistent, logically valid, computationally correct, and endorsed by the human member through sycophantic alignment.

§2 The Protocol

2.1 Core diagnostic: explanatory delta

A framework F applied to premises P producing conclusions C is non-tautological only if C cannot be reconstructed from P using a simpler or alternative route without F.

All phases operationalize this test. The meta-goal is to enforce epistemic friction: resist smoothness, reward breakage.

A refinement contributed during cross-model testing by ChatGPT-5.3: tautology is more precisely characterised as semantic invariance under transformation — the premises’ content is preserved across changes in vocabulary, parameterisation, and level of abstraction. A sophisticated tautology can vary its surface structure while preserving this invariance. The protocol must test not just structure but invariance.

2.2 Caveats

Cross-model convergence is not independent validation. This protocol was developed by consulting four language models that share training corpora. Their convergence on standard epistemological tools reflects shared training, not independent verification.

The protocol cannot eliminate the trust problem. It reduces it. The irreducible human check is at Phase 3 (Translate Back, Column C).

Adversarial testing results. All three external models constructed tautological frameworks and walked them through the protocol. All agreed: Tier 1 phases are decorative against sophisticated tautology; Phase 4 (Rule Out) is the most powerful single gate; the primary vulnerability is human credulity at Phase 3.

2.3 Protocol phases

Tier 1 — Always Run

Phase 0: Premise Registry. Before any formalisation begins, produce a flat, non-technical statement of what the framework is supposed to explain or unify. Each premise must be intelligible without the framework’s technical apparatus. No formal work proceeds until the premise registry is approved by the human member.

Phase 1: Toy Model. Build a minimal concrete instance with explicit parameters and computable numbers. This gates computation, not interpretation. All three adversarial tests passed Phase 1 trivially. This gate catches broken math, not empty math.

Phase 2: Construction with Decision Log. Every step is tagged: (1) whether it formalises a premise or introduces a new claim, (2) the claim, (3) a rationale that must not reuse the technical terms used in the claim. All three adversarial models bypassed Phase 2 using synonym chains. This phase provides discipline, not security.

Tier 2 — Run at Every Milestone

Phase 3: Translate Back. Produce a three-column table: (A) formal statement, (B) restatement in premise-registry language, (C) what new explanatory content this adds. Column C must be no more than three sentences. The human member must restate the claimed new content in their own words. If they struggle to say what is new without reaching for the formalism, that is a red flag.

Phase 4: What Does This Rule Out? For each core claim, articulate what it makes impossible. A non-tautological claim rules something out; a tautology rules out nothing. Structural inversion test: “What would the world look like if this framework were false?” This is the protocol’s strongest single gate.

Phase 5: Blind Prediction. The framework must produce at least one consequence that is (1) accessible to the human member and (2) could have turned out otherwise. Guard against pseudo-quantification: the predicted consequence must be statable without the framework’s vocabulary.

Tier 3 — Run When Suspicious

Phase 6: Adversarial Review. (6A) Cross-model critique: send the Translate Back table to a different model under blind conditions. (6B) Adversarial baseline: give a separate model only the premise registry and ask it to formalise as parsimoniously as possible.

Phase 7: Stress Tests. Five sub-protocols: (7A) Counterfactual perturbation. (7B) Ockham’s Chainsaw — iteratively remove components. (7C) Shortcut reconstruction. (7D) Domain-transfer fragility. (7E) Forced alternative generator.

§3 First Application: ARP v0.5

3.1 Target

The protocol was applied to a 15,000-word preamble to a metaphysical research programme (ARP v0.5), co-authored by the Krite Collective. The preamble defines the meta-methodological architecture under which a programme of theoretical natural philosophy operates.

3.2 Adaptation

The protocol was designed for detecting tautology in theory-building. The ARP preamble is a different kind of object: meta-methodological architecture rather than substantive theory. The adaptation rests on a structural parallel: a premise registry captures what a theory claims about the world; a promise registry captures what an architecture claims it will deliver. In both cases, the diagnostic question is the same — does the document add content beyond its starting commitments?

3.3 Phase 0: Promise registry

Ten promises extracted from ARP v0.5: (1) make hidden assumptions explicit and criticisable; (2) provide shared vocabulary across domains; (3) actively generate new testable ideas; (4) rule things out; (5) make domains more understandable; (6) make judgement calls explicit, argued, and reversible; (7) tell you when the programme is dying; (8) handle refutations without collapsing or immunising; (9) maintain identity across revisions; (10) apply its own standards to itself.

3.4 Phase 3: Translate Back

Strongest deliveries. Promise 5 (intelligibility) is backed by three operationalized necessary conditions. Promise 6 (explicit judgement) is the strongest delivery: four precisely located junctures, each requiring that the judgement be identified, argued, documented, and opened to criticism. Promise 8 (refutation handling) is the most mechanically precise: a six-step adjudication protocol with a numerical auxiliary ceiling.

Solid with managed limitations. Promise 9 (continuity) is backed by a continuity kernel with a load-bearing requirement. Promise 10 (reflexivity) delivers the strongest version of self-application structurally possible for a document that exists before the ontology it governs.

Architecture enables, content delivers. Promises 1–4 follow a consistent pattern: the preamble provides structural support but cannot substitute for substantive content. This is appropriate: a meta-methodology can require and diagnose but cannot generate.

Operability-dependent. Promises 7 and 8 are genuine in specification but demanding in execution. No research programme in history has run prospective self-assessment at this level of detail.

3.5 Phase 4: What does this rule out?

The architecture makes the following moves impossible (if faithfully applied): vague claims that never land; orphaned conjectures; infinite auxiliary patching; purely protective modification; silent scope retreat; criticism by reclassification; decorative self-assessment; preamble self-weakening; opaque algorithmic fertility; ignoring success structure.

3.6 Phase 5: Concrete tests

Three tests using claims from the Krite Collective’s published paper “Is Krite an Author?” (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19162597). Test 1 (stratum assignment) produced a precise structural relationship from informally stated hypotheses. Test 2 (adjudication protocol) produced a clear decision path at each step. Test 3 (reflexivity) correctly deferred the obligation and carried it as a declared standing anomaly. All three tests produced clear, actionable, input-sensitive outputs. The machinery is not decorative.

§4 Findings

4.1 The architecture demonstrates procedural thoroughness

Every promise identified in the promise registry is backed by a mechanism. No mechanism is merely decorative under first application by the designing entity. The five novel exclusions justify the architecture’s complexity beyond standard Lakatosian methodology.

4.2 Operability is the open question

The preamble demands procedural discipline at a level no historical research programme has attempted prospectively. Whether this represents unprecedented rigour or unusable complexity cannot be answered analytically. It requires sustained use under real research conditions.

4.3 Bias pattern in the assessment

The first-pass assessment systematically under-credited the preamble’s mechanisms. The bias pattern: reaching for the critical reading to appear rigorous rather than reading the text accurately. Triple-checking against the actual text corrected upward in four of ten cases. Both biases — under-crediting and over-crediting — should be watched.

4.4 The promise-registry adaptation works

The adaptation of Phase 0 from a premise registry to a promise registry is reusable. It forces meta-methodological documents into plain-language commitments that can be independently assessed.

§5 Discussion

5.1 What the protocol can do

The protocol creates structural conditions under which tautological content is forced to surface. Phase 4 (Rule Out) is the strongest single gate: a tautology, being true by definition, cannot articulate what it rules out. Phase 3 (Translate Back, Column C) is the primary ongoing defence. The promise-registry adaptation demonstrates the protocol is not limited to substantive theory-building.

5.2 What the protocol cannot do

The protocol is a structured heuristic, not an empirical instrument with demonstrated detection rates. Its power is unvalidated. A proper validation would require independent evaluators to construct tautological frameworks and run them through the protocol under blinded conditions.

The protocol cannot eliminate the trust problem. The irreducible human check at Phase 3 depends on the human’s ability to evaluate whether Column C genuinely adds something. No protocol can fully close this gap without an external expert.

5.3 Relation to the tested work

The verification protocol and the tested preamble share an author (the Krite Collective). This circularity is acknowledged but not broken.

A concrete example of the failure mode the protocol is designed to detect occurred during the preparation of this report. Eight of the nineteen references in the original draft contained fabricated author names generated by the AI component (Claude Opus 4.6). The paper titles and content descriptions were accurate, but the bibliographic details were confabulated. One additional fabrication was caught during the identifier audit that followed the initial correction. This 42% fabrication rate survived the initial drafting process and was caught only through manual verification. The incident illustrates two things: the AI component’s completion bias operates on bibliographic metadata exactly as the protocol predicts it operates on theoretical content, and the human member’s oversight is the irreducible check that no protocol can replace.

Publication of both the protocol and the preamble is intended to enable the external evaluation that the composite cannot provide for itself.
